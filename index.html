Trend · answer

Two product lines are scaling hard and falling hard — and Cost per Subscribe is up ~33%.

Portfolio S2S fell ~9.5pts over 8 weeks. Cost per submit held roughly flat ($229 → $262), which is why the earlier read of this data looked benign. But Cost per Subscribe — the metric that actually ties to subscription revenue — rose from ~$363 to ~$482 (+33%) over the same window. Disaggregated, the drag maps almost entirely to 3-in-1 Pill (spend scaled from $4K to $123K/week, 30x, while S2S fell 11 points) and Sex Rx + Testosterone Support (spend +40%, S2S -8pts). Climax Control / PE, Hard Mints, and Sex Chews are stable. The stacked spend chart below makes it visible: the red band (3-in-1 Pill) barely existed in week 1 and dominates by week 8.

Question 2 · answer

Five patterns that correlate with low S2S

The low-S2S ads cluster around five recurring patterns — product-line concentration, unsupported outcome framing, non-testimonial format skew, voice-persona over-indexing, and product-naming vagueness. These are correlations, not confirmed causes: product line, delivery context, and audience mix are confounded with each other, so isolating any single driver requires controlled testing. Still, these are the strongest signals in the data and the best starting points for action.

01

Concentrated on two product lines

3-in-1 Pill + Sex Rx + Testosterone account for the majority of low-S2S spend despite being a minority of total portfolio spend. The same physical offer framing (triple-stack outcome promise) appears disproportionately in the low cluster.

04

Young female voice (25–34) over-indexed in the low cluster

77% of low-cluster spend runs with a female 25–34 voice persona. This is heavily confounded with product line — the same persona works well on Climax Control but underperforms on 3-in-1 Pill and Sx+Testo. It may be a persona-product mismatch (young wife framing vs. an older buyer), or it may just be that anything on those product lines underperforms. The high cluster uses a more balanced persona mix, but that could also be a product-mix effect. Testing different personas on the same product line would isolate the signal.

Evidence loading…
05

Product-name clarity correlates with S2S — a hypothesis worth investigating

Climax Control has a name that tells you exactly what it does. Hard Mints are concrete and memorable. Both hold strong S2S. Meanwhile, "3-in-1 Pill" is described by ingredient count, not benefit, and "Testosterone Support" is vague about what testosterone does for sexual performance. The correlation between naming clarity and S2S is strong, but this is a hypothesis — not a proven cause. It's possible these products have worse S2S for reasons unrelated to naming (landing page flow, audience quality, subscription friction). Still, it's the most actionable structural pattern in the data: if the product name leaves too much for the landing page to close, creative can only do so much.

Diagnostic split between the two weak products. 3-in-1 Pill and Testo Support look similar on the cluster chart, but the pattern diverges on inspection — and the fix for each is different.

  • 3-in-1 Pill — branding problem, nothing more complicated. Same active ingredients and same use case as Hard Mints (63% S2S, $371 Cost per Subscribe) but runs at 48% / $527. The product is, functionally, Hard Mints in pill form — so the ~15-point S2S gap is most cleanly explained by naming. "Hard Mints" tells you what you get and what it does; "3-in-1 Pill" is described by ingredient count. Rename, and a lot of the gap likely closes.
  • Testo Support — positioning and CAC target, not naming. "Testosterone Support" tells the viewer exactly what it does — naming is not the weak link here. The harder issue is that the product behaves more like a daily-cadence ED supplement than a pure testosterone booster, so two things are likely true at once: (a) creative should lean into what the product is (ED meds with a daily shape) rather than a testosterone-first framing, which gives creators and scripts more honest ground to stand on; (b) the CAC target was set against on-demand ED economics rather than daily-supplement economics — recalibrating that target may account for a meaningful share of the gap on its own. Ro's Daily Rise Gummies is the clearest comp: same mechanism, daily shape, positioned accordingly.

Top high-S2S ads

Signal check

Same ad. Same creative. 38 points of S2S difference — and ~2x Cost per Subscribe.

These aren't two different executions — they're the same ad, running in different contexts. One lands at 78% S2S. The other drags at 44%. Cost per submit barely moves ($255 vs $289, ~13% gap) — which is what the earlier read of this deck pointed to. But Cost per Subscribe tells the real story: ~$327 vs ~$655, a 2x difference on identical creative. That means two things at once: (1) pausing by ad isn't as precise as pausing by placement or audience, because delivery context swings the same ad dramatically; and (2) even so, when these ads land in the wrong context the subscriber cost is genuinely uneconomic. The cleanest move is to look at the media settings behind the weak delivery — but in the meantime, pausing the worst low-S2S spenders is still the right call.

Actions · next steps

What to do next.

The pause call is right — Cost per Subscribe on the low-S2S cluster is 27% higher than on the high cluster, and rising. That's the immediate action. The remaining four items are what the portfolio looks like after the pause lands: one investigation (web funnel), one creative workstream, two product-level fixes, and one KPI direction for when the data infrastructure catches up.

0

Pause the worst low-S2S spenders on 3-in-1 Pill and Testo Support

Cluster math: $551 (low) vs. $433 (high) Cost per Subscribe — a 27% premium on the low side — and the weekly trend is up 33% over 8 weeks. Start with the top 5 low-S2S ads by spend (biggest dollar drag) and reallocate into the high-S2S cluster. MTA CAC by product line would firm the story up, but the subscribe-cost math is already enough to act on.

1

Investigate the web funnel

Cost per Subscribe rose across most product lines, not just the two weak ones — portfolio-wide CPS Subscribe is up ~33% in 8 weeks. If the drag were purely creative, it would concentrate on the worst performers; instead it shows up almost everywhere. Worth auditing the post-click experience: landing page flows, the subscription offer shape, any recent checkout changes, and the D0-to-subscribe handoff. Creative can only explain so much of a portfolio-wide drop.

2

Redo the creative plan for 3-in-1 Pill and Testo Support

Down-weight the non-testimonial formats (both run sub-42% S2S on these two products) and move budget into direct-to-camera testimonial with a 35+ voice and explicit proof rails — clinical citations, active-ingredient equivalence, partner-voice framing. Worth testing older and male voices on the same product lines to separate persona signal from product signal, since the young-female voice is over-indexed in the low cluster.

3

Product-level fixes — rename 3-in-1 Pill; reposition Testo Support

  1. Rename 3-in-1 Pill. Same active ingredients as Hard Mints, just in pill form. Hard Mints runs at 63% S2S / $371 Cost per Subscribe; 3-in-1 Pill at 48% / $527. A clearer name — something that tells the buyer what it does rather than counting ingredients — could close a meaningful share of that gap with no creative or media change required.
  2. Reposition Testo Support. The name is strong; the friction is that the product behaves more like a daily-cadence ED supplement than a pure testosterone booster. Two moves: (a) recalibrate the CAC target to a daily-supplement reference class instead of on-demand ED (this alone may account for a large share of the gap); (b) shift creative positioning toward the daily-cadence angle, which gives scripts more honest ground than a testosterone-first lead. Ro's Daily Rise Gummies is the comp.
4

Future KPI reframe — product-specific Cost per Subscribe targets (once FB ↔ S2S is wired up)

This assumes a data prerequisite that isn't fully in place yet — FB ad data isn't fully connected to D0 S2S in Looker, so this isn't something media can optimize against today. When it is, the direction worth discussing: shift the account-level KPI from Cost per Submit to Cost per Subscribe + HDYHAU, and give every product its own target rather than a blended 60% S2S floor. Hard Mints ($371) and Sex Chews ($342) run on different economics than Climax ($426), 3-in-1 ($527), or Testo ($476) — a single blended target under-rewards the naturally efficient products and unfairly penalizes the naturally higher-friction ones. Long-term, per-product pLTV-based ROAS would be the cleanest version of this, once subscription LTV is well measured.

Appendix

Data scope, definitions, caveats

Data: TS S2S AllUp (495 ads) and TS S2S Weekly (9 dates, 2/16–4/6), scoped to campaigns Zeus_20251114_Prospecting_Sales_ZeusASC-1DC and Zeus_20250110_Prospecting_Sales_Zeus_TubeScience. Creative features (motivator, hook archetype, visual format, voice persona, sales formula, transcript) are LLM-classified against TubeScience's canonical taxonomy. All aggregate S2S figures are spend-weighted, not simple means.

Cluster definition: "Low cluster" = ads with S2S below the portfolio spend-weighted average; "high cluster" = ads at or above. This is a different threshold from the 60% client benchmark shown in the KPIs above — the benchmark is the client's target floor, while the cluster split uses the portfolio's own center of gravity. Top-20 cards in each cluster are ranked by spend.

Product normalization: The classifier generated 93 unique product-name variants across 495 ads. These were normalized into 6 categories (3-in-1 Pill, Climax Control / PE, Hard Mints, Sex Chews, Sex Rx + Testosterone, Other) using keyword pattern matching. Spend was aggregated and S2S was re-weighted after normalization. Directional conclusions hold; exact spend splits should be treated as approximate.
